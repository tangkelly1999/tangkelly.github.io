DRIVER CONTROLS   

The Midnight Sun Solar Car Team has been designing and building solar-powered electric vehicles at the University of Waterloo since 1988. With our next car, MS XIV, we’re creating a car that is both energy-efficient and practical. Midnight Sun proudly represents our university at both the American Solar Challenge and World Solar Challenge.

The driver controls system consists of multiple boards separated into three types: The Driver Controls Carrier board, Pedal board, and the Control Stalk Interface board. The carrier board is the central hub for inputs and it transmits that information to the rest of the CAN network. It holds the controller board (which holds the STM32F0 MCU and CAN Trasciever). The pedal PCB fits near the brake and accelerator pedal. It takes in analog signals from the accelerator pedal and it hosts a magnetic sensor for brake pedal sensing. The Control Stalk Interface board inputs signals from the steering wheel, and controls turn signals, cruise control signals, and high beams.

My task:
We used I2C to communicate between boards last year for competition, but the boards were placed relatively far apart (e.g. the pedal board was half a meter away from the steering). That distance meant the signals were susceptible to EMI from our motors and walkie-talkies.

My focus was on trying to testing and reducing noise for the Steering system. For example, the turn signal stalk should output different voltages to the ADC, depending on if the right turn or left turn signal should be on. However, the noise from the walkie-talkies is so significant that our actual signals are almost irrecognizable.

MSXII Current Sense

The Midnight Sun Solar Car Team has been designing and building solar-powered electric vehicles at the University of Waterloo since 1988. With our next car, MS XIV, we’re creating a car that is both energy-efficient and practical. Midnight Sun proudly represents our university at both the American Solar Challenge and World Solar Challenge.

Current measurement is an essential requirement for our BMS (Battery Management System), as it will allow us to protect the battery from over-current conditions, calculate SOC (State of Charge), and monitor solar/grid charging. Being able to calculate SOC is quite important, so the more accurate we can measure current, the better we can calculate our power usages.

I am currently designing the 3rd revision for the current sense PCB. The issue with the previous board was that the ADC did not meet our desired accuracy and input range. Our new board will contain a 24-bit ADC with a 3V input range, so that we can accurately measure current ranging from -150A to +150A.
